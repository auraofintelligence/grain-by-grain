Lasting through anything · the rock from the sky

The rock we joke about

Most of us know this rock from the dinosaurs, or from a film where a great rock comes for the planet. It is the rarest bit of trouble on the whole list, and the one we tend to wave off because it feels made-up. That is the very reason to say it out loud. Leaving it off would be dodging the truth, and the kind part is this: getting ready for it asks almost nothing extra of us.

Why bother naming it

The one people brush off.

Picture a shooting star: a fleck of space dust burning up in a blink. That is the sky tossing tiny rocks at us most nights, and almost none of them matter. So why give this one a page of its own? Not to frighten anybody. It earns its place because it is the single trouble on the list that has cleared the board before, back when the dinosaurs were here. Leaving it out would put a hole in an otherwise straight story. So we name it, keep it in proportion, and carry on. That really is the whole ask.

What it actually is

Mostly harmless. Rarely not.

Known Speculative

Rocks from space hit the air all the time. Nearly every one is a grain or a pebble, flaring up as a shooting star and mostly burning out long before it reaches the ground. Bigger ones are rare, and they are real. In 2013 a rock about the size of a house came in over the city of Chelyabinsk in Russia and burst high in the sky. The blast shook windows out of their frames right across the city, the way a brutal hailstorm smashes glass, and by space standards that was a little one. The giant sort, the kind that finished off the dinosaurs, sits in a class of its own: so rare that across a whole human life it barely rates a thought.

The good news

We are not helpless now.

Known

Here is the part the films skip. We are not sitting ducks any more. Telescopes now sweep the night sky, mapping the big rocks and working out where they are headed years in advance. And in 2022 a NASA spacecraft called DART flew out and crashed into an asteroid on purpose, nudging its path by a measurable amount. So a big one caught early enough could be pushed gently off course long before it came near us. Think of a slow car rolling toward you from the far end of the street: you have all the time you need to notice it and step aside.

The odds, straight

Rare beyond worrying.

So where does this leave you on an ordinary Saturday? Just about nowhere. Measured against a single human life, the big civilisation-ender is so unlikely that it hardly earns a worry between now and old age. Far more of us will meet a flood, a fire or a big blow than will ever see this rock. Naming it is not about fearing the sky. It is about telling the whole story honestly, then getting back to the day.

Why it sits here

It comes almost free.

There is not much you can do about one that is already on top of you. But a safe place to shelter, and stored ways to keep food, water and power coming, are the very same kit you would want for a cyclone, a long blackout or a dry year. So this rare rock rides along almost for free with everything the island would put in place anyway. Have a look at that shared kit on the ready-for-anything page, and the idea of deep, calm rooms under the dunes on the underland page.

This is also where thinking in centuries starts to make sense. Most of us plan for next week, maybe next summer. A rock from the sky asks us to lift our eyes a little further and build a few things meant to stand for a very long time. Do that once and it holds for every kind of trouble, not just this one. When you are ready, step back and see them all together on the threats page.
