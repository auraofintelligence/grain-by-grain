Out of the dig · how the walls form

Real island builds from the sand

The sand from a tunnel could be pressed into blocks and pieces, then built into the real things the island needs: a place to wait for the bus, a better hall, a clubhouse, the bones of a home. Made here, out of what the ground already gives, instead of barged over on a truck.

What it could build

Things you could point at and use.

Modelled

Every build below is worked out from the sand a tunnel gives up. You can count the sand yourself and see the same numbers. None of it is dug yet, so read these as "one day, if the island chooses it", not as a job already under way. The bigger the build, the more of the dig it takes.

Covered bus stop

shade, a seat, a dry wait

A small shelter where the bus pulls in: a bit of shade, a seat or two, and a plinth ready for a battery and the cables that run to it. The kind of thing you could cast from island blocks and set down in a day.

  • about 1 tip-truck load of sand, the spoil from roughly 3 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Jetty footing set

solid footings for the jetty

The heavy caps and blocks a jetty sits on, tidy runs for the services, and a few spare and inspection pieces for later. Cast here, they would save a barge trip for every load. On the water, so only ever with the Quandamooka people's agreement.

  • about 5 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 10 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

School or community hall upgrade

a friendlier hall

Ramps for easy access, shaded edges to sit under, a bit of outdoor seating and some dry storage. Small pieces that could make a hall work harder for everyone who uses it.

  • about 7 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 14 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Small housing shell

the bones of a home

Not a whole house, but its core: a raised inner shell, the walls around the wet rooms, a spine to carry the pipes and wires, and porch pieces out front. The frame a home could grow around.

  • about 14 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 29 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Ballow Road sand-sports clubhouse

a home for the club

A clubhouse for the sand-sports mob at Ballow Road: walls, shade, a counter or two, storage for the gear, and plinths for a screen. Somewhere to gather before a game and yarn after it.

  • about 19 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 38 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Ferry terminal upgrade

a kinder arrival

The edges where people step off the ferry: public seats to wait on, bases for the lighting, clear wayfinding, and service corridors kept out of the weather. On the water, so only ever with the Quandamooka people's agreement.

  • about 29 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 57 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Stadium seating on a slope

seats built into the hill

Tiered seating stepped into a natural slope, so the land itself does half the work of holding people up. Cast the rows from island blocks and the hill carries the rest.

  • about 40 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 80 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

Rock wall or living edge

a coast that holds

A low wall, or a living, planted edge, to take the sting out of a big tide and give small sea life somewhere to settle. Anything on the shoreline would need the Quandamooka people's agreement first.

  • about 51 tip-truck loads of sand, the spoil from roughly 102 metres of a people-sized tunnel
    Modelled

First things first

Country comes before any of it.

None of this moves a grain until the fresh water is safe and the Quandamooka people, through QYAC, have a say. These are builds for the island's own good, its homes, its gathering places, its coast, not for export or profit, and the order matters: water and consent first, blocks a long way after.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches.
