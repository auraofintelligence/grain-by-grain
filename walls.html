First principles include the law

The walls, named.

This project works because it knows exactly where the walls are, what they're made of, and which ones are moving. Naming them is what lets the dreaming stay bold: each wall is where the on-screen work begins, what you can model and film long before anything is built, and every number here carries its receipts.

Wall 01

Mining ended by law.

Known

The North Stradbroke Island Protection and Sustainability Act 2011 phased sand mining out, and operations ceased in 2019. This project is not a mining proposal, and it doesn't want to be one: the whole thesis is that the island's next chapter is capability, not extraction.

Where the on-screen work begins: the material story runs as literacy and simulation. Learn what's in the sand, model what it could become, and, when the digital twin exists, test everything there first. The mining skills that lived here for eighty years are honoured as capability, carried by real people, not as a wound to reopen.

Wall 02

Nuclear power: a wall that's moving.

Known Modelled

Today, federal law prohibits nuclear power generation in Australia. But look at the wall closely and you can see it shifting: Australia mines and exports uranium, including to India under a civil nuclear cooperation agreement; AUKUS is bringing nuclear-propelled submarines and a whole trained nuclear workforce; and repeal of the ban is a live, recurring debate in federal politics. This is not a forever wall. It's a wall with a door being argued about.

So the move is readiness, not waiting. Here's what that means in plain words: the physics of a reactor can be studied, designed and safety-tested entirely in software, legally, today, by anyone. OpenMC is a free, open-source reactor physics code, the same kind of tool national laboratories use, and the island's monazite sands genuinely contain thorium. So a Straddie student can build a virtual thorium reactor now, stress-test it against real physics, publish the model for anyone to check, and be years ahead on the day the law changes. If it never changes, the learning still stands: reactor physics is energy literacy either way.

Receipts

  • Prohibition: Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 and Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Act 1998 (federal bans on nuclear power installations).
  • Uranium exports to India: Australia-India civil nuclear cooperation agreement (in force since 2015, first supply arrangements from 2017).
  • AUKUS: nuclear-propelled submarine pathway with Australian crews training in nuclear operations now.
  • Open tools: OpenMC (open-source Monte Carlo neutron transport, MIT/DOE lineage), free to learn and run.

Wall 03

Water: the wall with numbers on it.

Known

The island's freshwater lens supplies people and Country, and nothing on this ladder may risk it. But here is what the receipts say is already happening to it, and these numbers deserve to be far better known.

~20 ML/daypumped from the island's aquifer to mainland Redlands, reported as running since 1996
~7.3 GL/yearthe same take, counted annually: billions of litres, year after year
up to 60%of the Redlands mainland water supply drawn from Minjerribah's aquifer
~1.6 ML/daywhat the island community itself uses: the mainland draws over ten times more

In the mind's eye

Eight Olympic pools. Every day.

Known

A megalitre is a million litres, and an Olympic swimming pool holds about two and a half of them. So the mainland draw is eight Olympic pools every single day: one pool every three hours, around the clock, roughly 2,900 pools a year. The whole island community, meanwhile, runs on well under one pool a day.

Modelled

Now the money view, back-of-envelope on purpose. On a typical south-east Queensland bill, water costs a few dollars per thousand litres. Twenty million litres a day at those rates is roughly $60,000 to $90,000 worth of water leaving the island daily, call it $25 to $30 million a year at retail value. Which raises the receipts question that matters most: how much of that value comes back to the island as infrastructure, services or care for Country? If the answer exists, this site will publish it. If it can't be found, that silence is a finding too.

Read that contrast again. The island community lives on about a tenth of what leaves the island every day, under bulk-supply arrangements negotiated far from this beach, on Country whose Traditional Owners hold native title. Who signed, for how long, at what price, and what does Country get back? Those are fair questions for any community to ask about its own aquifer, and they sit squarely in the P4A gripe-seed territory: long-term contracts versus local sovereignty, measured against the best agreements Traditional Owners have won elsewhere (see People and trust).

Where the on-screen work begins: the aquifer twin would come first, before any machine is even sketched. An open groundwater model of the freshwater lens (MODFLOW, the standard open-source tool, with the island's published hydrology) would make every megalitre visible: what falls, what recharges, what's drawn, what leaves. You cannot negotiate what you cannot see. A twin like that is how the community could one day see.

Receipts (figures as reported 2019-2023; verify against current Seqwater data)

  • Redlands2030: Reduce water take from the Minjerribah aquifer (20 ML/day since 1996, 7.3 GL/yr, 60% of Redlands' needs, Redlands total ~40 ML/day)
  • Seqwater: North Stradbroke Island (Minjerribah) Drought Response Plan
  • Queensland Government ministerial statement: water supply certainty for North Straddie (island community use ~1.6 ML/day; ~25 ML/day for the SEQ grid)
  • Queensland Water Regulation Amendment 2023 impact analysis

Wall 04

Zoning: when the paper doesn't match the land.

Known

Every block of land on this island carries two descriptions: what it actually is, and what a planning map says it may be used for. Sometimes those two drift decades apart, and the paper wins by default. That's a real wall, and like the others it deserves to be named rather than grumbled about.

The worked example: a 1.37-hectare block at Amity Point, owned by a church. Its zoning permits one use, a place of worship, from a precinct plan written for another era. Its overlay maps stack up bushfire, wetland, coastal erosion, and a storm-tide flood line drawn for the year 2100. A church building once stood there, moved to the site to escape coastal erosion, and was then eaten by termites. Read all that at machine speed and you conclude that the land is useless. That conclusion is wrong, and it's worth being precise about why.

The overlays are maps of questions, not verdicts: they say "prove your design handles fire, water and time here", which is exactly what this ladder's method does for a living. And "worthless on paper" cuts the other way: land the market can't price is land a community can actually be given. Its true value isn't as loan collateral, and it never will be; it's use and meaning: a site for care, learning or community, held by people patient enough to build for the 2100 flood line instead of pretending it isn't there.

Where the on-screen work begins: the plan is to model the block honestly in the twin first. Which overlays actually bind, at what heights, for what uses; what a low-impact, flood-aware, fire-aware design looks like; what the land wants to be versus what the paper remembers it as. Rezoning is slow, political and real, and the strongest thing you can carry into that room is a model anyone can check.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches.
