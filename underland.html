The far horizon · back to the threats

The city under the dunes

The far end of this whole ladder is a question, not a promise. Could people one day live well in a cool, safe, self-contained place under the dunes: a home that could ride out the worst the world throws at it? It's a thought experiment, held lightly. Nothing here is a plan to dig. It's a picture worth testing.

Why go under at all

The ground keeps its own weather.

KnownSpeculative

A few steps underground, the temperature barely moves all year. It sits mild and steady, the way a wine cellar or a wombat's burrow stays cool through a summer scorcher and warm on a cold winter night, and it does that on almost no power. The same depth that steadies the air also puts a roof over you against fire, storm and searing heat. In the furthest reach of the dream, it could be shelter from the rare, once-in-an-age shocks set out on the threats page too.

A closed loop

Everything cycles, nothing is wasted.

ModelledSpeculative

Picture air, water, food and power all going round and round with nothing thrown away, the way a submarine, a spaceship or a sealed terrarium keeps its crew alive far from any help. None of that would be new here. It is the island's other pages gathered into one place: energy to run it, walls and water to hold it, the bench for food and making, and alchemy to close the last few gaps. Sort those out and the loop is most of the way there.

How you would actually know

You test it in a computer long before a shovel.

This is the honest part. You would not start by digging. You would build the whole thing first inside a computer and run it against every threat, over and over, until you either trusted it or found where it broke. Only if the simulation said it was truly worth it, and only if the community and the Quandamooka people chose it, would anyone move a grain of real sand. So this is not a plan to start digging. It is a plan to keep asking, carefully, whether digging would ever be the right call. The threats page is the test bench it would have to pass.

One shelter, many threats

The same safe place answers a lot at once.

Here is the quiet argument for it. A cool, sealed, self-contained place under the dunes does not solve one danger on its own; it answers a whole row of them together. Heat, fire, storm, a long stretch without the grid, the rare big shock: one shelter meets many of the threats in a single move. That is what makes a hard, far-off idea worth holding on to, and worth testing properly before anyone ever acts on it.
