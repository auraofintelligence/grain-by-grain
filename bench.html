The bench

Skill by skill, atom by atom. Every civilisation that ever lasted was built by people who knew their materials by hand. This rung is where that knowledge comes home: a bench, a hand lens, a jar of sand, and a community that repairs instead of replaces.

Sand literacy

This island is a materials course, free to enrol.

Known

Minjerribah's sands carry quartz, ilmenite, rutile, zircon and monazite: the feedstocks of glass, titanium, ceramics and rare-earth magnets. That's not speculation, it's the documented mineralogy that supported an industry here for eighty years, ending in 2019. The ore ships left; the knowledge doesn't have to. Learning to read the sand is the cheapest, most honest first rung of material sovereignty there is. The full stocktake, mineral by mineral and element by element, is on what the island is made of.

Small steps that add up

Learning that builds the thing it teaches.

The learning here breaks into small steps, each one small enough to finish in one sitting and each a real contribution. Small steps bundle into a finished thing, an object you made or a skill you can use, and those build up into something the island couldn't do before. A six-year-old sorting sand and an engineer checking a model are on the same ladder, doing the same kind of thing at different rungs.

Known Speculative

The maker-space itself is a proposed lab, not yet an operating workshop. The atoms above need nothing more than a table and supervision to start, and everything on the bench stays lawful, safe and small until real review says otherwise. The maker-space lab site holds the proposal, the tool-shed thinking and the working forms. And the tip loop is the other half of this rung: the island's richest ore arrives by truck, already refined, and gets buried for want of anyone to catch it.
