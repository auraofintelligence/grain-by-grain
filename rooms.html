Down in the tunnels · back to the tunnels

The hole and the building are one job

The clever idea in digging under a sand island is that the hole and the building are the same job. What you dig out becomes the walls; the tunnel you carve becomes the room. Nothing is thrown away.

The neat trick

The sand you dig becomes the wall.

Modelled

Dig a tunnel the normal way and you're left with a mountain of sand to get rid of, trucked off to sit in a spoil heap somewhere out of sight. Turn that on its head. The same sand you pull out could become the walls around you, the floor under your feet, even new land and reef offshore. You can count the sand a tunnel gives and see what it could turn into. It's the same thinking behind the island's full-spectrum alchemy and its reclaim-the-tip loop: nothing has to leave as waste if it can leave as something useful.

What it could become

Walls that grow out of the dig.

Here's how a wall might come together as the machine crawls forward. Some of it borrows from things that already stand up above ground; some is still only a drawing. The hardest one is right at the end.

Blocks that lock together, no cement

like LEGO, like drystone

Picture walls built from blocks that clip into each other, the way LEGO does, or the way an old drystone wall holds without a drop of mortar. Cut them so they interlock and the wall can shift a little in an earthquake and settle back instead of cracking. It's a real design idea people have drawn up, not something anyone has built at this size yet.

A glass skin on the walls

melt it where it stands

Now the ambitious part. Melt the sand of the wall right where it sits so it cools into a smooth, water-proof glass skin, like the glossy inside of a glass bottle. Lovely idea, very hard to do. It needs heat of around 1,700 degrees held steady behind a machine as it moves, and nobody has managed to run that continuously along a wall. This one is a long way off.

  • A smooth, sealed glass lining, poured in place
    Speculative

Keeping the water out

the unsolved bit

Whatever the wall is made of, it has to hold back wet sand and the water sitting in it. Sealing it properly, so the room stays dry for the long haul, is the hard part and the bit no one has fully cracked. This is where the plan is most careful and least sure of itself, and it's right to name that plainly.

Where it stands

Most of this is still on the drawing board.

None of this is dug yet. The plan is to test every part of it in simulation, on a computer, long before a single grain is moved in the real world, and to build only if the community and the Quandamooka people, through QYAC, choose it. This is deep-time, patient work: measured in careful years, not seasons, with plenty of room to walk it back or say no.
