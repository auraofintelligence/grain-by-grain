Rung 01 companion · the care

Care is the first infrastructure

Before the tunnels, before the energy mesh, before any of it: somebody is minding somebody. That work holds every community together, it is mostly unpaid, and it is exactly where a ladder like this one has to start telling the truth. So here is this page's one unbreakable rule: care gets mobilised and honoured, never packaged and sold. The money side has its proper doors, and we'll show you where they are. But nobody's nan is a revenue stream on this island.

Staving off dementia The rule, in full

The quiet emergency

Dementia, by the numbers.

Known

Not a rare disease. The second biggest cause of death in Australia, and the biggest for women. On an island of a couple of thousand people, the statistics say this is dozens of neighbours, and the people caring for them are mostly family, mostly unpaid, mostly women.

The good news is real

The island itself is the medicine.

Known

The World Health Organization publishes guidelines on reducing the risk of cognitive decline, and they read like a description of a good life on a small island: move your body, stay connected to people, keep learning, eat well, have a reason to get up. None of it is a pill. All of it is a place.

Which means every rung of this ladder quietly doubles as dementia prevention. An elder sorting minerals at the bench is doing cognitive engagement. A kid and a grandparent at the drawing board is social connection and purpose in one sitting. The film club, the tip-loop shed, the trust hub's paid roles: every one of them is the WHO list wearing work clothes. A community built for joyful responsible abundance turns out to be, almost by accident, a community built to keep its elders' minds.

And one idea from the corpus deserves its own light: the island's digital twin as memory medicine. Reminiscence therapy, revisiting the places and times of your life, is a standard, evidence-based part of dementia care. A faithful digital twin of Minjerribah, its beaches and jetties and streets, would let an elder who can no longer walk to Cylinder Beach stand there again. The evidence layer this ladder is already building for engineering reasons becomes, for free, a therapy tool for the people who love this place most.

The kink, ironed flat

Mobilise the care. Never monetise it.

Known

Fair warning about how this page got made: the research behind it was drafted with AI tools that tried, over and over, to turn the care economy into a product. Subscription nans. Tokenised kindness. Care as a funnel. Every draft, the same drift. So the rule got written down hard, and it has two halves that never mix:

Mobilise: the community side

The unpaid work that holds the place together, minding an elder, restoring a dune, showing a newcomer the ropes, gets counted and honoured, through things like the C-hour on the patient capital page. Honoured is the word. It is never a product, never speculated on, never sold back to the person who did it.

Fund: through the proper doors

Real money for care already exists and is meant to be spent: the NDIS, aged care funding, and the Support at Home scheme that funds up to $15,000 of assistive technology per person per year. Building good tools and getting them funded through those doors is honest work. That's monetisation in its correct place: the system pays for tools, the person never pays for love.

And the law just moved our way. The new Aged Care Act, in force since late 2025, makes "wellbeing, independence, autonomy" and reablement a legal duty of every provider, not a nice-to-have. Tools that genuinely restore capability and record a person's own choices are no longer a luxury pitch; they're how a provider proves it's obeying the law. That is the honest business case, and it needs no token attached.

Hardware honesty

The wellness gear, without the hype.

The corpus is full of ambitious wellness hardware, and this page keeps only what survives an honest read. Saunas and hyperbaric oxygen chambers are real therapies with real uses. But two lines get drawn in permanent ink:

Safety first, and it's not negotiable: Australia's medical regulator explicitly warns against hyperbaric chambers in homes, because a pressurised oxygen-rich room is a fire waiting for a spark. So any community-owned wellness gear on this ladder lives in properly equipped, professionally run facilities. The co-op can own the machine; the clinic runs it. Known

Claims stay labelled: hyperbaric therapy is proven medicine for specific conditions. The bigger longevity and cognition claims around it are an active research frontier with genuine dispute, and they wear that chip here rather than being sold as settled. Disputed

Where the money already is

The leak is bigger than the fix.

Known

Whenever someone says prevention is too expensive, show them the leak. Australia burns roughly $36.6 billion a year on wasted food, about $1,500 a year out of a young household's pocket, and $4.7 billion a year on the direct costs of dementia care, which prevention demonstrably reduces. The money for a caring, healthy community isn't missing. It's leaking out of a system that finds it easier to pay for the damage than the upkeep. Every dollar of surplus this ladder's enterprises make flows toward the locked fund, which is how upkeep finally gets a bank account.
