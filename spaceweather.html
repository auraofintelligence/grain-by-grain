Lasting through anything · space weather

Space weather: the storm on the sun

Think how much of your day leans on two things: the power grid, and anything with a chip in it. The lights and the fridge. The phone in your pocket. The eftpos at the shop, the pumps that push water up the hill. Now picture one event, from a long way off, that could knock the whole lot out at once. That is a big storm on the sun. It sounds like a film, but it is a disaster that insurers and governments already plan for with a straight face. So it is worth a calm, plain look.

What it is

A sunburn for the power grid.

Known

Every so often the sun throws out a huge blast of charged particles. Scientists call it a solar storm. Most of these miss us, or brush past politely. Once in a while a big one sweeps straight over Earth, and that is when it can overload power lines and fry satellites and the electronics we all live on. The easy way to picture it: a giant electrical surge, sent our way from space. A kind of sunburn, but for the power grid instead of your skin.

It has happened

The sun has done this before.

Known

In 1859 the biggest one on record swept past Earth. People call it the Carrington Event, after the astronomer who watched it happen. Telegraph wires, the texting of their day, sparked and gave their operators a shock. The aurora, those glowing curtains of light, lit up so brightly that people saw them down near the tropics, a long way from where they usually hang.

Closer to now: in March 1989 a smaller storm hit the power grid in Canada. It cut the electricity to the whole province of Quebec, millions of people, in about 90 seconds. That is quicker than boiling the kettle. These are not scare stories dreamed up to sell you something. They happened, and people wrote them down at the time.

The odds, plainly

How likely is the big one?

Disputed

Here is the honest shape of it. Small solar storms happen often, and do little more than paint a pretty aurora and wobble a compass needle. A monster like 1859 is much rarer, the sort of thing you might expect once in a very long stretch of time. Most scientists agree another big one will come one day; that part is a "when, not if". What they argue over, in good faith, is exactly how likely it is in our lifetime, and exactly how bad it would be. That argument is a healthy sign, not a worry. It is how careful people handle something real but uncertain: no need to lie awake over it, every reason to take it seriously.

Why the island cares

The island's weak spot, and the good news.

An island hanging off the end of a single power cable from the mainland is extra exposed. If a solar storm tips the mainland grid over, the island's lights would go dark with it, like being the last carriage on a train that has lost its engine.

But turn that around, and the same island has a quiet strength waiting. An island that could run on its own power, and stand on its own two feet for a while when the cable goes quiet, would be one of the safer places to be. Tuck away a few spare, protected bits of electronics, the way you keep a spare torch and batteries in a drawer, and a storm that fries the everyday gear does not leave you stranded.

And here is the kindest part: you get told it is coming. Satellites sit out in space watching the sun all day, and a big blast gives hours of warning before it reaches us. That is time to unplug the precious things and switch to your own power. The island's watchtower would do exactly this: watch the sky, and turn a wall of numbers into one plain sentence a person can act on.

None of this needs a special space-storm plan. The same simple kit that carries you through a cyclone or a long blackout carries you through this too: your own power, knowledge stored offline so it still works with the internet down, a few spare parts, and a plan the household actually knows. It is one kit for every trouble.

The bigger picture

Ready for the storm you might meet.

This is one of the dangers gathered on the threats page, sitting alongside the bushfire, the cyclone and the long blackout. The lovely thing is that the preparation is shared. Getting ready for the cyclone you will almost certainly meet one day also gets you ready for the space storm you might. You do the work once, for the trouble you can see coming, and you are quietly covered for the one you cannot.
