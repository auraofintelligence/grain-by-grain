What the sand could build for us

Dig under the island and it hands you back a great pile of sand. The usual answer is to cart it away. The better answer is to keep it here and build the things the island actually wants.

The simple idea

A dig gives back a pile of sand.

None of this is built. It is a set of ideas about where that sand could go instead of a spoil heap out of sight. Pick a family below to follow. Each one is a could, a would, a maybe one day, with more to wander into.

Six things it could make

What the sand could turn into.

Six families of build, each a signpost to follow further. Some borrow from things that already stand up and work in the world; some are still just a drawing. Take your pick.

Real island builds

the everyday things we ask for

A bus stop, a jetty, a school hall, homes, the Ballow Road clubhouse, the ferry terminal: the plain, useful buildings a small island keeps needing, made from its own sand.

Cleaning the bay

shellfish do the work

Oyster reefs that quietly filter the water as they feed, and give little fish a place to hide, feed and grow. A living thing doing a job for the whole bay.

  • clearer water and more fish in the shallows
    Known

Holding the coast

soft defences, not sea walls

Living shorelines, mangrove nurseries, care for the dunes, and quick repair after a storm. A coast that bends with the weather instead of breaking.

  • a shoreline that softens a storm and stays put
    Known

Better waves

shaped, then tested

Sand banks shaped for kinder, cleaner waves, tried out first as models on a computer long before a single grain is moved in the water.

  • a wave worth paddling out for, checked on a screen first
    Modelled

Places to gather

room to come together

Yarn circles, market rings, festival grounds, and open ground for celebrating Country. Somewhere to sit, trade, dance and mark the seasons.

  • a place for the community to meet and mark the seasons
    Known

Made for home

make it, mend it yourself

Tiles, pavers, benches and privacy screens that residents could make at home and fix themselves, from the same island sand, at a backyard scale.

Before any of it

Count the sand, and ask first.

You can count the sand a tunnel gives and see how far one dig would stretch across this list. This is for the island's own good, its homes, coast and gathering places, not for export or profit. And anything in the sea or on the shore needs the Quandamooka people's say, through QYAC. Nothing here is presumed.
