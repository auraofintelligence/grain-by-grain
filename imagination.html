Energy everywhere · the drawing board

Imagination is the only limitation

Nobody has thought of every way to catch energy. Not the engineers, not the professors, not the AI. So this is for the next inventor: draw your machine, any machine, and we'll run the quick honest numbers together. How much power might it make? What would it cost in energy and materials to build? And if it holds up: one day the maker-space's printers and cutters could turn drawings into things. One rule only, and it's not negotiable: nothing that could hurt a living creature.

The loop

From pencil to prototype in six steps.

This is how a sketch becomes a real thing, and every step is something a kid, a class or a curious adult can actually do this week.

StepWhat happensWho or what helps
1. Draw itPaper and pencil is perfect. Label what moves, what the energy pushes against, and where the power comes out.You. Maybe a mate.
2. Snap and describePhotograph the drawing and fill in the design brief below: what it catches, where it sits, what it's made of.The copyable brief on this page.
3. Quick numbersPaste the brief into an AI session (or ask at the bench). Minutes later: a power estimate, a materials list, and roughly how long the machine takes to pay back the energy used building it.Any AI assistant + the ballpark helper below.
4. Honest verdictThe design gets told how sure we can be about it: Known physics, Modelled, or Speculative. No design is laughed at; every design gets told the truth.How sure we are.
5. Build it smallPromising designs go to the maker-space bench: 3D printers and CNC cutters turn the drawing into a bathtub-scale prototype when the space comes online.The bench rung.
6. Film itWhatever happens, it's a story. Working or wonky, the test gets filmed, and the best ones could one day join an island cinema.The Screen lane.

Why this page exists

  • Because not all designs have been thought of, and the next one might be yours.
  • Because quick honest numbers beat polite silence: kids can handle "your machine makes three phone charges a day" and then make it better.
  • Because when the maker-space stands up its printers and cutters, drawings on this island stop being hypothetical.
