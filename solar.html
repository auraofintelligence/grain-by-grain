Energy everywhere · sunlight

Solar on what's already standing

Not one hectare of bush gets cleared for this. Every roof, carport, water tank, wall and road on the island is a candidate surface, already built, already there, doing nothing with the sun that lands on it every day. Here is the honest inventory of what's already standing.

The mind's eye first

What's actually on the roof.

Known

The sun delivers roughly 5 kWh to every square metre of north-facing surface here, most days. A single house roof, panelled properly, covers most of that household's own 16 kWh-a-day habit. Multiply that by every roof on the island and the number gets serious fast, without a single tree coming down.

The catalogue

Six surfaces already doing nothing with the sun.

Every one of these already exists on the island today. None of them needs a single new square metre of cleared land.

House and shed roofs

the obvious one, done properly

The biggest surface on the island by far, and the cheapest power going. This is where every household starts, and where most of the island's own daily habit gets covered.

  • Precedent → rooftop solar is standard, boring, proven technology across Queensland
    KnownCost keeps falling; this is the rung every other option is measured against.

Ferry and shop carpark shade

shade first, power second

A carpark canopy earns its keep twice: shade for cars and queuing passengers on a hot day, and a panel roof overhead the whole time. The ferry terminal's car park is the obvious first site.

  • Precedent → solar carports are common commercial installations on the mainland
    KnownDoubles as weather protection: a genuine two-for-one on a surface that already exists.

Rainwater tank lids and tank walls

a forgotten surface

Every backyard tank has a flat or curved top doing nothing, and often a north-facing wall. Small, but there are a lot of tanks, and every one of them already exists.

  • Status → simple flexible panel kits already exist for curved surfaces
    KnownA household project, not an infrastructure one: a good maker-space bench build.

Fence lines and boundary walls

vertical solar

Panels don't have to lie flat. Fitted vertically along a north-facing boundary fence, they catch morning and afternoon sun especially well, exactly when household demand peaks.

  • Precedent → vertical bifacial solar fencing is a real, growing product category overseas
    ModelledLess power per panel than a roof, but it catches the hours a roof-only system misses.

Road and path surfaces

the ambitious one

Specially engineered panels strong enough to be walked or driven over, turning a footpath or a quiet lane into a power surface. This is the still-being-researched one on the list.

  • Status → trialled overseas with mixed results; durability under real traffic is the open problem
    SpeculativeWorth a bench-scale footpath trial before anyone touches a road. Ties neatly to the footsteps lesson on the drawing board.

Concentrated solar heat

for the alchemy, not just the grid

Mirrors focusing sunlight onto a small point can reach temperatures hot enough to sinter sand or charge the thermal battery directly, sunlight straight into heat, no electricity in between.

  • Precedent → Kogan Creek Solar Boost, Queensland; Sundrop Farms' 115 m tower, South Australia
    KnownThe direct link to the alchemy page's thermal loop and the sand battery on the energy rung.

The standing rule

  • Zero hectares cleared, ever. If a design needs new land, it isn't on this page.
  • Existing structures first, always: a roof, a fence, a carpark, a tank, a path.
  • Shade and shelter count as a benefit in their own right, not just a power source.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches.
