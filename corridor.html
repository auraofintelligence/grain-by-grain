The ladder, laid on a map

The line on the ground

Every rung of this ladder eventually has to touch dirt somewhere real. This page is where it does. There's a rough line you can trace across Minjerribah, from the ferry where everyone arrives, past a workshop, to a wellness hub, a sports and story ground, and out to the old mine site: a corridor where the ideas become places. None of it is built yet, and none of it happens without Quandamooka Country leading. It's a shape to aim at, held lightly and honestly, one workable piece at a time.

Walk the line How it's held

Stop by stop

Five places, one thread.

Read these as a walk, not a blueprint. Each stop is a real spot on the island doing an honest job, and the thread between them is the same one that runs through the whole site: start where you're standing, do the easy reasonable thing first, keep the harder dreams on the Screen lane until they're ready.

Stop one · the front door Modelled

The Gumpi ferry terminal

Dunwich is where the island meets the mainland, and the terminal is the handshake. An upgrade here is the least exotic thing on the line and the most useful: a better arrival, a place that tells its own story well, room for people and freight to move without a scrum. It's the front door to everything downstream, and the existing ferry data lab is already thinking about the numbers behind it.

Stop two · the workshop Modelled

The Ballow Road hub

A short way on sits the working heart: a screen-and-sand hub where two kinds of making share a roof. One side is hands-on with the island's raw material, the sorting and testing bench that feeds the alchemy. The other is the screen craft, where the things that can't be built yet get filmed, modelled and imagined instead. It's where the Build lane and the Screen lane sit side by side, in one shed, on purpose.

Stop three · the wellness hub and the portal Speculative

The Amity community block

At Amity there's a vacant block, a bit over a hectare, that once held a small church building moved there years ago and later lost to termites. It's zoned for community facilities, exactly the health, care and learning uses this ladder is about. The idea for it is a wellness hub: the clear-head work, longevity and care-tech, and an outdoor cinema tied to the Quandamooka Film Festival so stories can carry the big picture in. It's low-lying ground near water, so anything built here rises above the flood line by design, not by wishful thinking. And in the long view, this is where a tunnel could open its mouth: the portal that links the corridor together underground.

Stop four · sport and story Speculative

The sand-sports and cinema ground

Near the wellness hub, a sports and recreation reserve could hold a beach-sports club and an open-air cinema: a place where the island plays, gathers and tells its stories out loud. It's council-managed ground, so it moves at the pace of proper community process, with QYAC as a central voice, not an afterthought. This is the joyful end of the corridor, the reason the rest is worth building: somewhere for a Saturday and a film night, that also quietly carries the deeper vision to anyone who turns up for the fun.

Stop five · the deep-time one Speculative

The old Point Lookout mine

At the far end sits the old mineral-sand mine near Point Lookout, land already being rehabilitated. The long dream for it is a place of learning: world languages, the advanced sciences, space engineering, wrapped around music festivals and celebrations of Country. And the honest way to build it is inside out, from underground up, the slow way, over a horizon measured in decades. This one leans hardest on things that don't exist yet: trust that has to be earned and legal barriers that have to be lifted first. So it lives mostly on the Screen lane for now, a destination the corridor points at, not a promise about next year.

What moves along it

A corridor carries more than cars.

The line isn't just a road between buildings. Four things travel it, and each one is a rung of this ladder wearing walking shoes.

People

  1. From the ferry to a job, a therapy, a game, a class.
  2. Skills and roles held by locals, through the trust hub.
  3. The whole point: a reason to arrive and a reason to stay.

Sand

  1. Dig a tunnel and you make spoil: clean sand with nowhere to go.
  2. That spoil becomes new ground and artificial reef, placed outside the reserves and only where the sea Country custodians agree.
  3. Nothing dug is wasted; the hole and the reef are the same project. Modelled

Stories

  1. The outdoor cinema and the film festival lower the doorway.
  2. Big ideas ride in on a good night out, not a lecture.
  3. Country, told by its own people, first.

Care

  1. The wellness hub feeds the island's aged-care and dementia support.
  2. Funded honestly through the proper doors.
  3. Built for the care networks already here, on their terms.

Whose, and how

Held by the co-op, steered by Country.

Modelled

A corridor like this only stays good if the community owns it. The frame is the Ready Set Co-op and a community sovereign wealth fund: the island's own vehicle for holding island assets, land, the hub, the energy, the sports ground, so the value made here stays here. The patient capital page shows how a fund like that gets seeded and structured without selling the place off to do it.

And the honest part, said plainly: the plans are further along than the asking. Sketching a corridor is the easy, joyful half. Walking into rooms and inviting people to build it is the hard half, and it's the half that turns a map into a place. Most of what's drawn here is easy, reasonable, and already happening somewhere in Australia and the world; it just needs the first real conversations, with QYAC and the island's own institutions leading, to begin.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches. Minjerribah is Quandamooka Country; QYAC is the public doorway and no endorsement is implied.
