The far horizon · the progress dial

A fuel gauge for the whole island

It would help to see, in one glance, how the island is going at making and using its own power, and looking after itself well. Not to win anything. Just to watch ourselves get a little steadier, season by season. You already know the feeling: it is the fuel gauge in the car, or that little solar app on your phone that shows how much power the roof made today against how much the house used.

The plain idea

One glance at how we are going.

Known

Scientists have a tidy way of measuring how well a place uses the energy around it. They call it the Kardashev scale, after the fellow who first dreamt it up, and the big milestone on it is a place making full, gentle use of its own patch of sunlight, wind and sea. The island's dial would be a plain-language version of that: how much of its own power it makes, how little it lets go to waste, and how well it could look after itself if the cable to the mainland were ever cut.

The heart of it

A dashboard, not a scoreboard.

This is the part to hold onto: it is a dashboard, not a scoreboard. It is not about growth, or money, or getting one over the next town along. It is simply the island keeping an eye on its own progress toward standing on its own two feet, gently and joyfully, the way you might watch a garden slowly come good. Higher on the dial is not winning. It just means a bit steadier, and a bit kinder to the place.

What it would show

A few plain gauges, side by side.

Speculative

Picture a handful of honest gauges. Power made against power used. Waste heat drifting down toward nothing, which is the whole idea behind the alchemy page. Water and food we can count on. And a simple one that anyone would understand: how many days the island could keep running on its own if it had to. Real numbers, plainly drawn, with nobody dressing them up.

Keep going

Nothing here is built yet.

None of this exists yet, and that is fine. The dial is really just a friendly way to keep everyone pulling in the one direction, and to notice the quiet wins as they come. If you want the how behind it, that lives on the method. And the everyday tools that would one day read these gauges for us are over on the smart tools.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches.
