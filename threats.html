Rung 06 · the great filters

The threats, all of them

Most places prepare for one disaster at a time and argue about the rest. Here we do something different: name every threat honestly, from the bushfire that will certainly come back to the rock from the sky that almost certainly won't come soon, and shows the punchline underneath them all. The same handful of preparations serves every single one. You never have to win an argument about the scary ones to justify the work, because the cyclone you'll definitely meet already justifies it.

Start shallow, go as deep as you're game. Every threat gets its local memory, its straight odds, its hardening, and its honest label. The deep ones also get the film they deserve.

Bushfire

this decade · certain to return

An island of eucalypt and heath in a drying climate. Fire is not an if.

  • Local memory → the 2014 fire burned more than half this island
    KnownStraight odds: recurring, guaranteed. Hardening: protection zones, stored water and pumps that don't need the grid, an island-mode microgrid, and rooms that don't burn. Mind's eye: in 2014 the burnt country was thousands of footy fields. Underground rooms, when they exist, are the one shelter fire cannot reach.

Cyclone and storm surge

this decade · climate-loaded

The big wind and the sea shoved ashore under it. This coast's oldest visitor, getting angrier.

  • Local memory → Cyclone Alfred crossed this exact patch of coast in 2025
    KnownStraight odds: recurring; warming seas push them further south. Hardening: dunes and living reefs as the shock absorber, storm-lock harvesters that drink the surge instead of dying in it, buried services, hardened power. Mind's eye: a big surge puts an extra metre or two of ocean on top of the tide, arriving at highway speed in gusts.

Drought and water stress

this decade · slow-motion

The disaster that doesn't roar. The sky just stops, and the lens keeps being pumped.

  • Local memory → the Millennium Drought emptied south-east Queensland's dams to near-empty within living memory
    KnownStraight odds: certain to recur. Hardening: desalination from sun and sea, tanks everywhere, and the aquifer twin making every megalitre visible while eight Olympic pools a day leave for the mainland (receipts on the walls page). Mind's eye: a household that stores one rainwater tank buys itself weeks of independence.

Supply-chain severance

this decade · proven in 2020

Nearly everything this island touches came over on a barge. Now imagine the barges stop.

  • Local memory → 2020 to 2022: empty shelves and year-long waits, worldwide, from one shock
    KnownStraight odds: shocks recurring; an island feels them first and worst. Hardening: the whole material sovereignty ladder: repair culture, local fabrication, stores of what can't be made, and the alchemy page's answer that the sand itself is a warehouse. Mind's eye: count today's meals, medicines and parts; almost every one crossed the bay on a boat.

Pandemic isolation

this decade · rehearsed already

The threat where the island's separation becomes its superpower, if the basics are local.

  • Local memory → 2020: this community lived a version of it like everyone else
    KnownStraight odds: epidemiologists treat the next one as when, not if. Hardening: local food and energy, telehealth that works offline-first, kiosks with knowledge cached, and trust between neighbours, which every pandemic study calls the real infrastructure. Mind's eye: an island that can feed and power itself turns quarantine from a siege into a quiet season.

Grid collapse

this decade · one storm away

The mainland's electricity fails, and the island's lights are just the last car in that crash.

  • Local memory → South Australia, 2016: an entire state black in an afternoon
    KnownStraight odds: demonstrated, repeatedly, worldwide. Hardening: an island-mode microgrid with its own generation and the sand battery holding weeks of heat; disaster kiosks as powered lighthouses in every township. Mind's eye: a household is about 16 kWh a day; a warm silo of sand can carry a township's essentials through the dark fortnight.

Carrington-class solar storm

this century · consensus-priced

The sun clears its throat and the world's transformers, satellites and grids catch the blast. This is the threat the reinsurers already price.

  • Local memory → 1859 set the benchmark; 2012 was a near miss; May 2024's storm lit auroras over Australia as a small reminder
    KnownStraight odds: serious studies put a Carrington-scale hit at rough single-digit odds per decade; not rare enough to ignore. Hardening: island-mode drills, spare transformers, hardened and spare electronics, open forecast models watching the sun, and everything on the grid-collapse card held longer. Mind's eye: the 1859 event electrified telegraph lines; today the same punch lands on everything with a chip in it.

East-coast tsunami

this century · quiet but real

Rare on this coast, but the continental slope offshore has slumped before, and the ocean remembers how.

  • Local memory → geologists map old submarine landslides down Australia's eastern slope; the size of past waves is argued, the slides are not
    Known DisputedStraight odds: low per year, never zero; Australia runs a national tsunami warning centre for a reason. Hardening: know your height above the sea, clear vertical evacuation, drills, and sealed refuges. The dunes are the ally: this island carries natural high ground everywhere. Mind's eye: most tsunami deaths happen below ten metres of elevation; the island's dune spine sits comfortably above that almost everywhere.

Nuclear war

this century · human-made

The only threat on this page humanity built itself and keeps pointed at itself.

  • The record → used twice in 1945; Cold War near-misses are documented history, including the night one duty officer declined to believe his own warning screens
    KnownStraight odds: rises and falls with politics, not geology, which means it can also be lowered on purpose. Hardening: distance from likely targets is the island's quiet advantage, then the same five preparations with fallout-aware sheltering and stored clean water. The people rung matters most: communities that hold together, recover. This ladder is peace-first by design: it builds shelters, never sides.

AI takeover and robot conflict

this century · argued by its own builders

The people building the smartest systems on Earth disagree, in public, about whether those systems could slip everyone's control. That open disagreement is itself the warning label, and honesty is the only credible position available.

  • The record → no precedent yet, which is exactly the point; expert forecasts run from "manageable" to "existential"
    Disputed SpeculativeHardening: happens to be precisely the island's digital architecture: sovereign, local-first, offline-capable systems with humans holding the keys; models you can run, read and switch off; no single dependence on a distant cloud. A community that owns its tools cannot be held hostage by them. And the drawing-board rule applies to machines too: nothing that can injure a living creature.

Geomagnetic excursion

deep time · the long version

Every so often, on geological clocks, Earth's magnetic shield sags for centuries. The last big one was about 42,000 years ago.

  • The record → the Laschamps excursion is solid science, and published research has argued it coincided with megafauna extinctions and the fading of the Neanderthals; how hard the effects bite is exactly the live argument
    Known DisputedExcursions sometimes keep company with extinction events; whether that's coincidence or mechanism is the frontier fight, and the comfortable answer doesn't get picked here by default. In the deeper model, a sagging field also unlocks the crust, which is where this card joins hands with the mega-tsunami one. Straight odds: unknowable on a human calendar. Hardening: the honest beauty of this one is that it needs nothing new; it is the Carrington kit held for longer, plus shelter and stored capability. That's the threat-agnostic thesis proving itself.

Mega-tsunami

deep time · proven, timing unknown

Not a theory. In 1958 a landslide in Lituya Bay, Alaska drove water 524 metres up the opposite shore, and fishermen anchored in the bay rode the wave and lived to describe it.

  • The record → Lituya Bay 1958, witnessed and measured; the Storegga slide put whole North Sea coastlines under about 8,000 years ago; Hunga Tonga erupted from underwater in 2022 and sent tsunami waves across the entire Pacific
    Known DisputedAnd it has more than one trigger: great submarine landslides, sub-ocean supervolcanoes (new ones are still being discovered on the seafloor), asteroid strikes into open ocean, and, in the frontier model, the crustal shifts of a geomagnetic excursion setting the great waves loose. Straight odds: the phenomenon is proven; the timing is the honest unknown, and it may never be predictable, but early warning is a live engineering question, not a fantasy: slope monitoring, seafloor sensors and deep-ocean buoys already watch for the ordinary kind, and the island's hydrophone ambitions point the same way. Hardening: elevation first, then the subterranean thesis in full: a sealed refuge inside a 200-metre dune does not care how big the wave was once it has passed. This threat is half the reason the tunnels rung points where it points.

Supervolcano

deep time · proven, monitored

Every few tens of thousands of years, somewhere on Earth, an eruption a hundred times anything in living memory dims the sky for years.

  • The record → Toba, about 74,000 years ago, left ash across half of Asia; the one under strain right now is Campi Flegrei, the restless caldera beneath the suburbs of Naples with half a million people living inside it
    KnownYellowstone and Taupo get the documentaries; Campi Flegrei gets the seismographs, swarming with quakes and slowly lifting the ground it holds. And the seafloor keeps surprising us: newly discovered submarine supervolcanoes tie this card straight to the mega-tsunami one. Straight odds: deep-time rare globally, but "which one and when" is a live question, not a comfortable one. The danger for this island isn't the blast; it's years of dimmed sun and failed harvests worldwide. Hardening: stored food, protected growing, and energy that doesn't need clear skies: banked heat in the sand and the swell both keep working under an ash-dimmed sky, which is a quiet argument for the whole energy mix.

Snowball Earth

deep time · it happened before

The rock record says this planet has iced over nearly pole to pole before. Slowly, but all the way.

  • The record → the Cryogenian glaciations, around 700 million years ago, are mainstream geology; what starts and ends them is still argued
    Known DisputedStraight odds: the deepest deep time on this page; nothing on a human calendar. It earns its card because it's the purest test of the thesis: a civilisation that can live warm under the sand on banked energy while the surface freezes has already answered every smaller cold snap for free.

Asteroid or comet

deep time · the rock from the sky

The one everybody jokes about until they remember it's the only disaster with a 100% historical extinction record.

  • The record → Tunguska 1908 flattened a forest; Chelyabinsk 2013 arrived unannounced; DART 2022 proved deflection is real engineering
    KnownStraight odds: city-killers are deep-time rare, and detection improves yearly. Hardening: deflection belongs to the world; the island's share is the universal kit: shelter, stores, energy, knowledge, and the capability to rebuild, which is the Kardashev conversation in disguise. Mind's eye: Chelyabinsk was a rock the size of a house; nobody saw it coming because it came from the sun's direction.

Grey goo

deep time · the runaway machine

Self-replicating machines small enough to eat the world and dumb enough to do it: the nightmare coined at the birth of nanotechnology.

  • The record → a thought experiment taken seriously enough that physicists have formally bounded it; nothing like it exists yet
    SpeculativeStraight odds: far off; out-replicating biology is monstrously hard, and bacteria have had four billion years of practice. Hardening: the honest card on the page: shelter helps least here and prevention is everything. Open, inspectable fabrication, the maker-space ethic scaled up, beats secret laboratories, which makes the bench rung part of the answer.

Non-human intelligence and contact

this century · disclosure is underway

This stopped being a fringe topic in front of the world's cameras. The United States Congress has held UAP disclosure hearings, officers have testified under oath about non-human intelligence programs, and the government's own investigation office logs cases it cannot explain. Leaving this card off would be dodging the truth, so it stays.

  • The record → congressional hearings and sworn testimony since 2022; official reports acknowledging unexplained objects; and the deep-time dataset humanity has always carried: gods, sky beings, ancestors and daemons in every culture's stories on Earth, including this Country's
    Known DisputedWhat's Known: the disclosure process, the testimony, the unexplained cases, and the worldwide mythological record. What's Disputed: what it all is, and everything about intent. Straight odds: genuinely unknowable, which cuts both ways and always has. Hardening: watch honestly with open instruments (the island's sensor mesh and hydrophones count), keep systems that work without anyone's permission, and stay humble. The story world kept a chair at this table long before Congress pulled one up.

The solar micro-nova

deep time · the frontier's biggest warning

Could the sun itself periodically erupt: smaller than a supernova, vastly bigger than any flare on record, enough to scorch a hemisphere, collapse the field and set the oceans loose? Of everything on this page, this may be the threat that deserves to rank first, and it is taken that seriously here.

  • The record → the word "micronova" was carried for years by frontier researchers, Ben Davidson foremost, before astronomy formally adopted it in 2022 for bursts observed on other stars; sun-like stars demonstrably superflare; and the argued fingerprints of a rough 6,000 and 12,000-year rhythm run through ice cores, magnetic excursions and the catastrophe myths of every culture on Earth
    Speculative DisputedThe proposed engine, in plain words: the galaxy has weather too. Charged, dusty waves sweep the galactic disc the way sector boundaries sweep past Earth in the solar wind, oscillating the charge environment of every star system they cross; stars respond by building energy, and periodic outbursts may be how a galaxy moves that energy outward at all. Mainstream astrophysics does not accept a solar cycle of this kind; the frontier says the evidence keeps mounting, and careful attempts to disprove it keep coming up short. That standoff is exactly what a shared, testable sandbox is for: turn it into a runnable model, feed it the ice cores and the star surveys, and let anyone, believer or sceptic, run the same numbers. Timing and cycle remain speculative, by the frontier's own admission. The preparation does not: early warning means watching the sun and the heliosphere with open models that improve every year, and hardening means everything on this page at once: the Carrington kit, the excursion kit, deep shelter, stored capability, and in the fullest version, the evacuation drills and subterranean refuges this whole ladder climbs toward. Whichever way the science lands, the work was never wasted.

  • The honest line → the story world runs the scenarios the spreadsheets can't
    FictionalThe mega-wave film, the century of aurora, the frozen surface with warm rooms beneath it: each one doubles as a drill, a recruitment poster and a record of what this community chose to take seriously. The film festival and the documentary builders are the production line.

How to read this page

  • Each threat is labelled honestly: Known phenomena carry receipts; Disputed and Speculative say exactly what is argued and where the frontier sits. No natural hazard here is ever called fiction; the Fictional chip belongs to the films alone.
  • Likelihoods are plain-spoken bands, not false precision. "This decade" means expect it; "this century" means plan for it; "deep time" means build a civilisation that outlasts the question.
  • Deep pages per threat will come as the site grows; this is the honest index of all of them.
  • Warning comes before hardening. The watchtower is the honest state of the art on what can actually be seen coming, and how far ahead.

Underneath all of them

Entropy, wearing costumes.

Known

Strip the costume off every card above and the same face looks back: disorder. Life in this universe is a constant ebb and flow of expansion and contraction, reproduction and consumption, and we have tamed one thin, precious slice of it. The deal has no pause button. The moment a civilisation stops organising, entropy resumes consuming its systems, its knowledge and its people, no cataclysm required: rust, rot, forgetting and drift do it for free.

That is not doom talk; it is the job description. Every rung of this ladder, the trust, the bench, the tunnels, the alchemy that refuses to waste a single grain, is organisation practised on purpose, joyfully, one generation handing the work to the next. Joyful responsible abundance is what winning slowly looks like.

Grain by grain, rung by rung. Not an official project page: a public spine for a family of open workbenches.
